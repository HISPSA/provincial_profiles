1.   Demographic Information

Eastern Cape Province is one of nine provinces in South Africa. It is divided into 7 Districts and two metropolitan municipalities: Nelson Mandela Bay Metropolitan and Buffalo City Metropolitan municipality Districts, Alfred Nzo, Amatole, Cacadu, Chris Hani, OR Tambo and Joe Gqabi Districts.

Domestically, it borders the following provinces: Western Cape to the west; Northern Cape  to the northwest; Free State to the north and  KwaZulu-Natal to the far northeast

The Eastern Cape is one of the poorest provinces in South Africa. This is largely due to the poverty found in the former homelands, where subsistence agriculture predominates

The province has a total population of 6,692,804 with a population density of 40/Km2

As far as access to basic services is concerned, 22.2% of households do not have access to piped water and 35.2% are without access to improved sanitation. In terms of housing, 35.9% of the population lives in informal dwellings or squatter settlements. Furthermore, 25% of households do not have access to electricity for lighting purposes and 56.6 % of households in the district do not have formal refuse removal by a local authority or private company (Census 2011).

According to the Census 2011 data 11.4% % of the household heads is unemployed (Unemployment rate as per official definition) with 33.6% of the Household heads in the district employed. Nearly a half (49 %) of the household heads has indicated that they fall under the category classified as not economically active.

Although the employment rate is 33.6%, 21.6% of households live with an annual income below R4, 800 or less than R400 per month. The majority of households’ annual income is between R4, 801-R38, 200 (52.9%).

A low percentage of the population (6.6 %) has no schooling in the Eastern Cape Province. Approximately (5.1%) of the people has some sort of formal education; 15.5 % has between grade 1 and grade 3; 20.0 % has between grade 4 and grade 7; almost a quarter (20.8 %) has between grades 6-8; whilst 11.5% has completed matric.

More than 80% of the households in the Eastern Cape Province have between 1 and 5 members (81.2%), with 41.9 % of the households in the district has only one or two members. More than two per cent (2.7%) of households has more than 10 household members. More than 10 per cent (16.2%) of the households consists of between 6 and 9 people

3.3.    Hospital Performance

3.3.1.    District Hospital Performance

District hospital performance on 4 key indicators is displayed in the table below. Usable bed utilisation measures the occupancy of district hospital beds, namely the proportion of usable beds occupied over the year, and therefore measures how efficiently a hospital is using its available capacity. Bed utilisation rate (BUR) should be read in conjunction with the average length of stay (ALOS). If a low ALOS occurs in conjunction with a high bed utilisation rate (>90%), this suggests that the hospital has a high demand for beds.

A very high bed utilisation rate (BUR) suggests that the quality of care provided to the patients may be compromised due to insufficient staff to provide optimal care to patients or patients might get discharged before optimal recovery due to the high demand for beds. A very low BUR may suggest that the hospital is under-utilised either because there is no need for the service in the area, or because patients choose not to use the hospital.

The average of the BUR rates for the Eastern Cape Province’s district hospitals is on a decreasing trend from 2011/12 to 2014/15 financial year. The Provincial average of the district hospitals (59.0%) was lower than national (65.8%) average in 2014/15

The average length of stay (ALOS) indicator measures how many days (on average) each patient spends in hospital. It measures aspects of the quality of care and efficiency of the hospital. If the ALOS is persistently high it suggests that patients spend too much time in hospital either because they are not timeously discharged or not appropriately treated resulting in longer recovery times, or they are not discharged when they should be often due to shortage of doctors in a hospital. Admission, treatment and discharge procedures should therefore be reviewed. If the ALOS is persistently low (less than 1.5 days), it could mean that patients are discharged earlier than they should be, or referral rates to other hospitals are high.

The ALOS in the Eastern Cape Province had been higher than the National averages the past 4 financial years.

The Caesarean section (C-section) rate is an important indicator of access to essential (and emergency) obstetric care and is one of the key maternal health indicators. It is also an important indicator that contributes to the measurement of the quality of maternal and neonatal care.

The caesarean section rate for the Eastern Cape district hospitals (20.6%) was below the average for all the district hospitals (22.7%) in the 2014/15 financial year. The Provincial rates were consistently lower than the national rates in the past 4 financial years.


 

The maternal mortality in facility ratio (MMR) measures the number of women who died in hospital or delivery facilities as a result of childbearing, during pregnancy or within 42 days of delivery or termination of pregnancy, per 100,000 live births in facility.  The MMR for the Province in the 2014/15 financial year was 76 deaths per 100 000 live birth, which was below the National average of 88 deaths per 100 000 live births.

 


Indicators values in black font are within an acceptable range from national and provincial average. Indicator values highlighted in yellow are considerably below or above the national average and should raise concern.

3.4.    Trend of Public Health Expenditure

The PHC expenditure per patient visit indicator measures the average cost of a patient visit to a primary care facility. In practice it is the average cost to the health service of a patient visit to a community health centre (CHC), clinic, satellite clinic or mobile clinic, excluding district hospitals but including the cost of managing the district. This indicator’s numerator is thus the total cost in a particular district of running all these facilities for a year. The denominator is the total PHC headcount for these facilities for the same year. It does not take into account the patient case mix found in practice.

The cost per PHC patient visit in the Eastern Cape Province has steadily increased over the years, with a slight decrease in the 2009/10 financial year. The cost per patient visit in the Eastern Cape Province was lower than national average from 2005/06 to 2014/15 financial years.

.

 

PHC (non-hospital) expenditure per capita, uses a subset of total PHC expenditure; most importantly it excludes DHS expenditure on HIV, nutrition, coroner services and district hospitals. The Eastern Cape Province’s PHC expenditure per capita expenditure showed an increase where the expenditure almost doubled from R421 in 2005/06 to 835 in 2014/15. It remained below the national average for the same period.

The District Health Services (DHS) expenditure per capita refers to the total expenditure on DHS, including the expenditure of local government (LG).

The Eastern Cape Province’s DHS expenditure steadily increased over the years and was above the National average since 2005/06 to 2014/15.

The indicator measures the average cost per patient, per day, seen at a district hospital. The indicator value is calculated by dividing the total expenditure of the hospital by the patient day equivalent (PDE). The PDE is calculated by adding the number of inpatients plus 1/2 of day patients plus 1/3 of outpatient and emergency room visits. This indicator measures how the resources available to hospitals are being spent and is a marker of the efficiency of the hospital as a whole.  The expenditure per PDE for district hospitals in the Eastern Cape increased over the years and was below the National average from 2007/08 to 2014/15 financial years

3.5.    Trend of Health Services Delivery 

The primary health care (PHC) utilisation rate indicators measures the average number of PHC visits per person per year to a public PHC facility. It is calculated by dividing the PHC total annual headcount by the total catchment population. The target for the South African public health sector is 3.5 PHC visits per person per year.

The PHC utilisation rate in the Eastern Cape has consistently remained above the national average for the past five financial years.

The PHC Utilisation rate under 5 years in the Eastern Cape remained at an average of 4 visits per year for the past five financial years and remained below the national average for the same period.

The supervisory visit rate remained at 78% for the past two financial years and slightly above the national average of 75% in the 2014/15 finanacial year.

4.   Burden of Disease

Years of Life Lost (YLLs) are an estimate of premature mortality based on the age at death and thus highlight the causes of death that should be targeted for prevention.  The four leading single causes of YLLs in South Africa were TB, pneumonia, diarrhoea and HIV related. As these are all linked to HIV it suggests that HIV-related mortality is by far the leading cause of YLLs in the majority of districts in South Africa.

4.1.    Life expectancy at birth

According to StatsSA midyear population estimates 2015, the average provincial life expectancy at birth has increased for both males and females in the Eastern Cape and will increase further to 53.7 years and 59.3 years for males and females respectively for the period 2011-2016.

4.2.    Chronic diseases

According to the 2015 General Household Survey 24.2% of the total population in the Eastern Cape suffers from chronic disease of which Hypertension (8.2%), Diabetes (3.1%) and Arthritis (2.8%) are the most prevalent.

4.3.    Assistive devices and chronic medicine

According to the 2015 General Household Survey an estimated 7.6% of the total population 5 years and older use assistive devices and 14.7% chronic medicine in the Eastern Cape.

4.4.    Fertility

According to StatsSA midyear population estimates 2015, the fertility rate in the Eastern Cape is the highest in the country, but has declined since 2001 and will decline further to 2.72 avergae number of children per woman for the period 2011-2016.


 

In the Eastern Cape Province, the five leading causes of YLLs were: Tuberculosis (12.7%), Other forms of heart disease (4.6%) Cerebrovascular diseases (4.3%) Influenza and pneumonia (4.3%), Intestinal infectious disease (3.8%).Tuberculosis as the leading causes of YLL in this province is significantly higher than all the other diseases.

4.5.    Child Health

Diarrhoea and Pneumonia are leading causes of death among children in SA. The diarrhoea with dehydration incidence rate in children under 5 years decreased significantly in 2011 and 2012 with the introduction of Rota Virus vaccination, but increased slightly above the national average in 2013 and 2014.

The incidence of pneumonia in children under 5 years, in Eastern Cape Province decreased significantly since 2008 to coincide with the introduction of the Pneumococcal vaccine and remained below the national average of 50 per 1000 population in 2014 .

 

The severe malnutrition incidence for children under 5 years, in Eastern Cape Province decreased significantly in line with the national average over the years, but increased above the national average in 2013 and 2014.

Immunisation coverage can serve as an indicator of a health system’s capacity to deliver essential services to the most vulnerable members of a population. The Measles coverage figures are also used to report on Target 4A of MDG4 which is to reduce the under-five mortality rate.

The measles 1st dose coverage in Eastern Cape Province has fluctuated during the last eleven years and remained slightly below the national average of 92% in 2014.

The Child under 1 year mortality in facility rate is a proxy indicator for the population based infant mortality rate, aimed at monitoring trends in health facilities between official surveys. The indicator measures admitted children under 1 year who died per estimated 1000 live births. The child mortality in facility rate for the province was significantly higher than the national from 2004 to 2014 and is on an increasing trend.

4.6.    Maternal and Woman’s Health

Antenatal coverage monitors to what extent antenatal services are reaching pregnant women. It measures the percentage of pregnant women that attend an antenatal clinic in a health care facility at least once during her pregnancy and is proxy indicator for MDG 5b for measuring access to reproductive health services.

The Antenatal coverage in Eastern Cape Province decreased in in 2012 and 2013 and was on par with the national average in 2014.

Early booking is very important especially for PMTCT.The Antenatal visits before 20 weeks rate in the Eastern Cape Province has shown a steady increased since 2006 but remained below the national average of 53% in 2014.

The delivery rate in facility indicator measures the proportion of all deliveries that take place in public health facilities under the supervision of trained personnel. The indicator serves as a proxy measure of access to public sector facilities and the measure of utilisation of these facilities by pregnant women and is used to track improvements in maternal health as part of Millennium Development Goal 5.

The delivery in facility rate in the Eastern Cape was on an increasing trend since 2010 however below the national average of 85% in 2014.

The couple year protection rate is a composite indicator of the different contraceptive methods. It reflects the availability, accessibility and acceptability of reproductive health services and serves as proxy indicator for MDG 5b.

The couple year protection rate increased over the years, but did not increase as significant as the national average in 2013 and 2014.

The perinatal mortality in facility rate measures the number of stillbirths and deaths in the first week of life in a health facility per 1,000 estimated live births.  The perinatal period commences at 28 weeks of gestation and ends seven completed days after birth. Mortality rates in the perinatal period are used to evaluate the outcome of pregnancy and monitor the quality of perinatal (prenatal and neonatal) care. The perinatal mortality rate increased above the national average from 2011 to 2013 and was slightly below the national average in 2014.

The maternal mortality in facility ratio is a proxy indicator for the population based maternal mortality ratio, aimed at monitoring trends in health facilities between official surveys. The indicator measures woman who died in hospital as a result of child bearing, during pregnancy, termination of pregnancy or within 42 days of delivery per 100,000 live births in facility. The maternal mortality rate fluctuated and was significantly above the national average of 132 deaths per 100 000 live births in 2014.

4.7.    HIV/AIDS and TB

The TB programme aims to reduce the pool of infected people in South Africa. The strategy employed to do this, attempts to prevent transmission of TB and to cure those who have already contracted the disease. There are two important indicators that are used to measure the success of this strategy namely Smear conversion rate and TB cure rate.

The TB Cure rate in the Eastern Cape Province has shown a steady increase since 2004 but remains below the national average. The cure rate for 2013 is 71% and is below national target of 85%.

The HIV prevalence in the Eastern Cape Province shows an increase from 21.7 in 2000 to 29.1 in 2012. The prevalence in the province is sligly lower than the national HIV prevalence.


4     Glossary

District boundaries and maps

Geographic information from the Municipal Demarcation Board is used to define District and provincial boundaries and is the same as is followed by the DHIS.

 

Averages

It is important to note that all averages (provincial, national, District and ISRDP) are weighted averages, based on the total numerator and denominator for all the Sub-areas included, and are thus not averages of the District indicator values.

Financial year and calendar year

Some indicators are displayed for (April – March), which is the financial year of the Department of Health. Indicators for financial years are annotated as 2012/13 for example. Other sources such as the TB data from ETR.net, antenatal HIV survey, water quality and cause of death data cover a calendar year (January – December). Data from StatsSA surveys are for the period of the census or survey.

Finance indicators

All expenditure trends over time used from the DHB have been adjusted for inflation, and figures are quoted in real 2014/15 prices, unless indicated otherwise.

Utilisation

ALOS: Average length of stay (District hospitals)

The average number of patient days that an admitted patient spends in hospital before separation. If the ALOS is persistently high it suggests that patients spend too much time in hospital either because they are not timeously discharged or appropriately treated resulting in longer recovery times, or they are not discharged when they should be. Admission, treatment and discharge procedures should therefore be reviewed. If the ALOS is persistently low (less than 1.5 days), it could mean that patients are discharged earlier than they should be, or referral rates to other hospitals are high.

BUR: Usable bed utilisation rate (District hospitals)

The number of patient days during the reporting period, expressed as a percentage of the sum of the daily number of useable beds. (Comment: The calculation here is an approximation - it assumes (1) a day patient occupies a bed for half a day, (2) there are always 30 days in a month. A very high bed utilisation rate (BUR) suggests that the hospital is very busy and that the quality of care provided to the patients may be compromised due to insufficient staff to provide optimal care to patients. A very low BUR may suggest that the hospital is under-utilised either because there is no need for the service in the area, or because patients choose not to use the hospital.
